Diagnosing Depression:

Statistical Linkages to Mental Illness via Single-Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs)

Summary

After learning about loved ones who had suffered from mental illness, I was determined to learn more about this 'invisible epidemic.' I learned that today's psychological treatments involve a lot of subjectivity and inconsistency when it comes to making diagnoses. Mood disorders such as depression and bipolar disorder have similar, overlapping symptoms to anxiety disorders and even learning disorders such as attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD). I chose to focus on three such disorders: depression, bipolar disorder and ADHD. The purpose of my research was to see if there is an underlying genetic difference between the three disorders that can be scanned for via Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs) and from there, further explained. This has implications for genetic screening to create more accurate diagnoses, and advances mental disorder research by providing the exact pinpoint from which to develop treatments.


Terminology


What is a Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP)?
A SNP (pronounced 'snip') is a single base change in a single strand of DNA, resulting in a base pair change in a DNA double helix. The four nucleotide bases are Adenine, Cytosine, Guanine and Thymine, each with unique chemical properties. 6-carbon ringed pyrimidines include Cytosine and Thymine, and 2-ring fused purines include Adenine and Guanine. Together these bases are complementary, which means that pyrimidines pair with purines: C and G pair up, and A and T pair up in double-stranded DNA. If a base pair changes from the average genotype, genetic variations may occur.


What happens when someone has SNPs in their genetic code?
Most of the human population has some variation between genetic code—after all, that's why we all look and behave differently! According to research from the NIH, humans have an average of 10 million SNPs in their DNA, one occurring every 300 nucleotides. SNPs occur most frequently in non-coding DNA regions (regions between genes) and act as biological markers to help scientists locate genes associated with disease. When SNPs occur within genes or in regulatory regions, they can cause a more direct role in disease. If a SNP results in an amino acid different from the average human genome, protein structure may change. It is precisely a series of SNPs on vulnerable animo acids that leads to malformation of proteins, which makes it difficult for ribosomes to carry out the correct sequence, and for enzymes or drugs to bind to the protein. Researchers have found that SNPs may predict an individual's risk of developing certain diseases or their response to drugs, the latter of which constitutes a field called pharmacogenomics which I hope to expand on.

Results

I focused on a few genes known to be linked to depression as noted from the Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man (OMIM) website. These included:

  • 5-HTT: regulates serotonin transmission
  • FKBP5: major depressive disorder, accelerated response to antidepressant drug treatment
  • TPH2: susceptibility to unipolar depression
  • MDD1: major depressive disorder 1
  • MDD2: major depressive disorder 2
  • HTR2A: major depressive disorder, in response to citalopram therapy

Conclusion

I compiled statistics on whether the amino acids affected by SNPs within the genes in question were harmful to protein function or not. Using a site called SNPs3D, I was able to view a relative measure of how damaging single SNPs are on a protein. Negative red numbers indicate a significant amount of damage, while blue numbers indicate a relatively benign SNP.

Then I used a software called RasPro to rasterize the proteins generated by the SNPs and amino acids of interest. This generated a three-dimensional protein domain model, showing secondary and tertiary structure. Areas negatively affected by SNP changes were highlighted in yellow or red, so I could get a sense of what the distorted protein looked like.

Lastly, I used SMART BLAST software to establish a sequence --> structure --> function model of each SNP of interest. I obtained the nucleotide sequence of the specific gene that contains the SNP using a tool called UniProt, then pasted this sequence into BLAST's SMART sequencing tool, which generates a gene map with proteins mapped onto specific loci as shown below. This gives a relative positioning of each protein along the gene and eventually the chromosome, which is useful for visualization.
